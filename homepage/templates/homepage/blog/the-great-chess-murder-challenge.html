The Great Chess Murder Challenge

22 September 2023

Imagine you are at home one evening and the phone is ringing. You answer the phone and the person on the other side of the line introduces himself as an inspector from the homicide squad, who has a few questions for you. This will hopefully never happen to most of us. But I am pretty sure, that in such a case most of us will shocked and speechless.

Apparently it had happened to English grandmaster Raymond Keene thirty years ago. I recently found an article, where he shared that story. It is a fantastic read with a lot of twists and turns.

The English police had arrested a man, who was the main suspect in the disappearance (and suspected murder) of his girl friend. In his prison cell he had written cryptic messages on the wall, which the inspector described as chess diagrams and chess notation. As Raymond Keene was well known because of his chess column in the Times, the inspector asked for his support.

Raymon Keene agreed and received shortly afterwards the above mentioned diagrams and notation. These proved not to be classical chess diagrams and notations (look at the image), but nevertheless Keene's was already hooked to the case and started working on the problem. In the best Sherlock Holmes approach he was able to work out most the mystery, but not all. So there is still some mysteries to be solved in this story.
